Product Teardown

Google Play Store
Is Designed for
the Wrong User

By Prithvik T Hanson    Grade 10    Aspiring Product Manager    May 2026
Overview

What is the Play Store?

Google Play Store is Android's primary app marketplace. Over 2.5 billion users open it to find, download, and manage apps. The job is simple: help people find the right app quickly.

It's not doing that well. Here's what I noticed.

The Problem

The Play Store is designed for children. But it already has a kids app.

Google Play for Kids already exists. It's a separate app built specifically for children, with parental controls and a safe browsing experience. So why does the main Play Store still feel cluttered, loud, and game-heavy? Its audience is adults now. The product hasn't caught up.

The homepage is dominated by games. Banners fight for your attention. Reviews are buried so far down you have to scroll to find them. It feels built to grab eyeballs, not to help a busy adult find a productivity app in 30 seconds.

Right now
Games pushed heavily on the homepage
Reviews buried below the fold
Cluttered, attention-grabbing layout
Recommendations feel generic
Kids and adults using the same UI
What it should be
Games in their own section only
Reviews visible immediately
Clean, utility-first layout
Recommendations based on actual usage
Kids fully moved to Play for Kids
Why It Exists

Google built the solution but never committed to it.

Play for Kids exists, but it's not pre-installed on Android devices, not marketed to parents, and not pushed as the default experience for young users. So kids keep ending up on the main Play Store, and Google keeps designing for them there.

This isn't really a design problem. It's a strategy problem. Google needs to fully commit to the separation, not just build the kids app and leave it sitting there.

My Solution

Three changes that would fix this.

📱

Pre-install Play for Kids on every Android device

Ship it alongside the main Play Store by default. Introduce it to parents during device setup. Once kids move there, the main app's audience becomes clearly adult and the design can finally reflect that.

🎯

Move games to their own section

Games don't need to disappear. They need their own home that isn't the first thing every adult sees. The default homepage should show utility apps, productivity tools, and socials. Recommendations should come from your actual Google usage data, not generic trending lists.

💳

Family payment controls with biometric approval

Parents set a monthly spending limit on their child's account. Any purchase needs a PIN or biometric approval from the parent's phone. No more surprise charges, no more kids using a saved card without permission. This alone would make Play for Kids the obvious choice for families.

Tradeoffs

What Google would be giving up.

Upside

A cleaner adult UI means people actually find what they're looking for faster. Better experience, more downloads.

Risk

Moving games off the homepage could hurt impulse game downloads and dent short-term revenue.

Upside

Payment controls build parental trust and give families a real reason to put their kids on Play for Kids for good.

Risk

Pre-installing Play for Kids adds to device storage. Small issue, but real on budget Android phones.

Upside

Personalised recommendations using Google's existing data is low engineering effort. The data is already there.

Risk

Biometric approval adds a step to purchases. Some friction is good for kids, but it might reduce adult in-app purchases too.

Impact

Why this matters.

The Play Store is the front door to the entire Android ecosystem. When that door is cluttered and confusing, people trust it less, find apps less easily, and bounce faster.

A Play Store built for adults, with kids fully served somewhere better, doesn't just improve the UX. It makes Android feel more mature and more useful for the people actually spending money on it.

Google already built the solution. They just haven't followed through on it. That's the whole problem.
