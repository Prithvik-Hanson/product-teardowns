Product Teardown

Google Play Store
Is Designed for
the Wrong User

By Prithvik T Hanson  ·  Grade 10  ·  Aspiring Product Manager  ·  May 2026
01 — Overview

What is the Play Store?

Google Play Store is Android's primary app distribution platform — the place over 2.5 billion users go to find, download, and manage apps. Its core job is helping users find the right app quickly and confidently.

It's not doing that job well. Here's why.

02 — The Problem

The Play Store is designed for children. But it already has a kids app.

Google Play for Kids already exists — a separate, dedicated app for children to discover games safely, with parental controls built in. So why does the main Play Store still feel cluttered, attention-grabbing, and game-heavy? Its primary audience is now adults. It's not behaving like it.

The main Play Store's layout is loud. Games dominate the homepage. Banners compete for attention. Reviews are buried below the fold. The experience feels designed to grab eyeballs — not help a busy adult find a productivity app in 30 seconds.

✗ Current Experience
Games promoted heavily on homepage
Reviews buried — requires scrolling
Cluttered, attention-seeking layout
Recommendations feel generic
Kids and adults share the same UI
✓ What It Should Be
Games in their own section only
Reviews surfaced immediately
Clean, utility-first layout
Personalised by your actual usage data
Kids fully redirected to Play for Kids
03 — Why It Exists

Google hasn't fully committed to the separation.

Google Play for Kids exists but isn't defaultly installed on every Android device, isn't heavily marketed, and isn't the default experience for young users. So kids still end up on the main Play Store — which means Google keeps designing for them there too.

The fix isn't just a UI redesign. It's a strategic commitment: make Play for Kids the default for children, market it aggressively, and free the main Play Store to serve adults properly.

04 — My Solution

Three changes. One cleaner product.

📱

Default install Play for Kids on every Android device

Ship Google Play for Kids pre-installed alongside the main Play Store on every Android device. Market it actively to parents during device setup. Once kids migrate there, the main app's audience becomes clearly adult — and the UI can finally reflect that.

🎯

Declutter the main app — games go to their own section

Games don't disappear — they get their own dedicated section, not prime homepage real estate. The default homepage shows utility apps, productivity tools, and socials. Recommendations are driven by your actual Google search and usage data, not generic trending lists.

💳

Family payment controls — spending limits with biometric approval

Parents can set monthly spending limits on a child's account for in-app purchases. Any purchase requires either PIN or biometric approval from the parent's device. No more stolen credit cards. No more surprise charges. This feature would make Play for Kids dramatically more trustworthy — and drive more parents to put their kids there permanently.

05 — Tradeoffs

What this costs and risks.

✓ Upside

Cleaner adult UI improves app discovery and time-on-task for the majority of users.

✗ Risk

Moving games off the homepage could reduce impulse game downloads and hurt revenue short term.

✓ Upside

Family payment controls increase parental trust — more parents actively choose Play for Kids.

✗ Risk

Default installing Play for Kids increases device storage footprint — minor but real.

✓ Upside

Personalised recommendations using existing Google data is low engineering lift — the data already exists.

✗ Risk

Biometric payment approval adds friction to purchases — could reduce overall in-app purchase volume.

06 — Impact

Why this matters.

The Play Store is the front door to the entire Android ecosystem. When that front door is cluttered and confusing, users trust it less, find apps less easily, and engage with it less confidently.

A Play Store designed clearly for adults — with kids fully served elsewhere — doesn't just improve UX. It makes the entire Android platform feel more mature, more trustworthy, and more useful.

Google already built the solution for kids. They just haven't committed to it fully. This is a strategic problem disguised as a design problem.
